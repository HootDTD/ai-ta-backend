# Concept-map-based assessment & automated concept-map mining

Research memo, 2026-07-07. Angle: scoring student concept maps against expert maps (Novak/Ruiz-Primo tradition), and NLP pipelines that mine concept maps / knowledge structure from student free text. Framing question: what does this literature teach us about maximizing resolver RECALL when aligning noisy student explanations to a small canonical reference graph?

## Key findings

1. **The validated classic score IS our coverage metric — "convergence score."** Ruiz-Primo, Schultz, Li & Shavelson defined three criterion-map-based scores: *proposition accuracy* (sum of per-proposition quality ratings), *convergence* (proportion of criterion-map propositions present in the student map — structurally identical to our node/edge coverage), and *salience* (accurate propositions / total student propositions). Interrater reliability across score types was high (>.90); convergence and proposition-accuracy were consistent across mapping techniques while salience was unstable; map scores correlated only moderately (r≈.31) with multiple-choice tests, i.e. they measure a partially distinct construct. (https://www.researchgate.net/publication/343975475_Comparison_of_the_reliability_and_validity_of_scores_from_two_concept-mapping_techniques) Relevance: our node/edge coverage design is the field-standard formulation; the field's reliability numbers set a realistic calibration target.
2. **Proposition-level scoring against a master map is the most reliable of all schemes.** McClure, Sonak & Suen (JRST 1999) compared six scoring methods; reliabilities ranged .23–.76, with the highest for proposition-by-proposition evaluation; master-map similarity correlations supported validity for 5/6 methods. (https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/(SICI)1098-2736(199904)36:4%3C475::AID-TEA5%3E3.0.CO;2-O) Relevance: don't score holistically or structurally; per-proposition credit vs a reference is the right unit — and human raters use graded quality judgments, not exact matching.
3. **Partial credit per proposition is standard.** Yin, Vanides, Ruiz-Primo, Ayala & Shavelson (2005) rated each student proposition on a multi-level quality rubric (four quality levels) rather than binary correct/incorrect; CRESST work applied Generalizability theory to these scores. (https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED483390.pdf ; https://cresst.org/wp-content/uploads/R640.pdf) Relevance: our binary resolved/unresolved per node throws away the graded-credit machinery that made these assessments reliable; a per-node partial-credit scale (fully taught / partially taught / mentioned) is prior-art-sanctioned.
4. **Constraining the response format raises reliability sharply (G-theory evidence).** Yin & Shavelson (CRESST R640, 2004): construct-a-map with *selected* linking phrases (closed set) had higher G coefficients than with *created* linking phrases, needing fewer propositions to reach a target dependability. (https://cresst.org/wp-content/uploads/R640.pdf) Relevance: the alignment problem shrinks when elicitation is constrained — supports offering canonical phrasings during the dialogue (menus, clarification confirms) rather than solving unconstrained alignment post-hoc.
5. **Kit-Build: the field's cleanest answer to alignment is to eliminate it by design.** Hirashima et al.'s Kit-Build gives learners the decomposed parts (node/link labels) of the expert "goal map"; because every component is shared, diagnosis is exact proposition-level diffing, fully automatic, with similarity scores 0–1 and difference maps. (https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s41039-015-0018-9 ; https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6294198/) Relevance: our clarification-loop direction (Apollo asks a follow-up presenting a canonical formulation, student confirms/rejects) is exactly a just-in-time Kit-Build move — prior-art-validated as the recall fix. Caveat: Kit-Build acknowledges it only reaches recall/understand levels of Bloom's taxonomy.
6. **Pre-LLM automated text→structure pipelines never parsed triples — they used fixed term lists + co-occurrence.** ALA-Reader (Clariana) takes ~30 preselected key terms, scans student text sentence-by-sentence, records pairwise term co-occurrence into a proximity array, prunes via Pathfinder networks, and scores percent agreement with an expert referent. (https://edutechwiki.unige.ch/en/ALA-Reader ; https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/A-computer-based-approach-for-translating-text-into-Clariana-Koul/2300ad895ee1b3e04648dff7edd1725af902d8fa) Reported in the Clariana line of work: ALA-Reader scores correlated r≈.71 with human raters, beating an LSA-based commercial scorer (r≈.56). Its web successor GIKS converts essays to knowledge-structure networks against a teacher referent in real time and is still actively studied (2025 structural-feedback study: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09892-0). Relevance: **edges were credited from sentence-window co-occurrence of two recognized terms, never from a parsed relational statement** — our rule requiring both endpoints resolved inside parsed candidate statements compounds node-recall loss quadratically for no prior-art reason.
7. **Model-based assessment suites (HIMATT / T-MITOCAR / AKOVIA, Ifenthaler & Pirnay-Dummer) automated text→model comparison with multiple similarity measures.** AKOVIA generates seven quantitative similarity measures (surface, graphical/structural, gamma, concept matching, propositional matching, balanced semantic matching) from natural-language input, with published reliability/validity testing. (https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10758-014-9224-6 ; https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/AKOVIA:-Automated-Knowledge-Visualization-and-Ifenthaler/44aac317edea7daff3b2b70962f9b25049b974dd) Relevance: report a measure VECTOR (separate concept-match and proposition-match scores), not one gate; the tools themselves appear unmaintained (methodology is the adoptable part).
8. **Open-vocabulary concept-map extraction from text is still unsolved in the LLM era.** LDK 2025 ("Automated Concept Map Extraction from Text"): prior work sat below F1 20%; their neuro-symbolic pipeline (spaCy + fine-tuned REBEL triple extraction + summarization) hit METEOR F1 25.7 and GPT-3.5 decomposed prompting 28.5 — the current SOTA on the WIKI benchmark (Falke & Gurevych's corpus). (https://aclanthology.org/2025.ldk-1.10/ ; https://aclanthology.org/D17-1320/) Relevance: pivoting to "extract a full student map, then graph-match" would import an unsolved problem upstream of grading; closed-set resolution against a known 5-node reference is the tractable formulation and we should keep it.
9. **Closed-set proposition matching with a plain bi-encoder reaches near-perfect recall.** A 2025 study matched student propositions to a teacher reference map via cosine similarity of IndoBERT embeddings at threshold 0.7: accuracy 96.3%, precision 96.2%, **recall 100%**, F1 98% vs expert conceptual-equivalence labels (27 student maps, Indonesian). (https://doi.org/10.1109/bts-i2c67944.2025.11399388) Small-n and one language, but it is direct evidence that embedding similarity against reference propositions is a high-recall tier — cheaper and apparently more recall-friendly than NLI tuned precision-first.
10. **LLM/RAG grading of concept-map propositions agrees poorly with experts.** Maskur et al. (Kinetik, 2026): best config (FAISS retrieval + GPT) achieved only Macro-F1 0.338 and QWK 0.146 against expert judgment; small-LLM configs were worse (Macro-F1 0.167–0.221, QWK≈0). (https://kinetik.umm.ac.id/index.php/kinetik/article/view/2594) Relevance: independent corroboration that an LLM judge over propositions is not a trustworthy score source — matches our transcript-audit over-crediting finding.
11. **Chunk granularity is a recall lever.** BEA 2025 LLM concept-mapping study (GPT-4o-mini): paragraph-level processing gave recall 74.5% of educationally relevant concepts vs section-level precision 83.6% — smaller input units + aggregation raise recall. (https://aclanthology.org/anthology-files/pdf/bea/2025.bea-1.58.pdf) Relevance: run the resolver per-turn / per-utterance-window and union the evidence, rather than over whole-transcript parses.
12. **Whole-map embedding similarity works only for coarse triage.** Sciarrone & Temperini (2024) validated InferSent/USE map-similarity measures against teacher judgments — useful for dashboards, but gives no node-level diagnosis. (https://doi.org/10.3390/electronics13091756) Hierarchy-aware partial-credit graph scoring vs a reference (GSKCM, 2025) proportionally reduces a concept's score when its neighborhood deviates. (https://accentsjournals.org/PaperDirectory/Journal/IJATEE/2025/4/8.pdf)

## Adoptable artifacts

- **UKPLab/emnlp2017-cmapsum-corpus** (https://github.com/UKPLab/emnlp2017-cmapsum-corpus) — Falke & Gurevych concept-map benchmark corpus + evaluation protocol (proposition-level METEOR/ROUGE matching with human maps). Apache-2.0 code, data under separate license; stable/archived. Adoption: borrow their proposition-matching evaluation protocol (strict/METEOR/ROUGE match levels) to score OUR resolver against human-labeled transcript propositions.
- **Babelscape/REBEL** (https://github.com/Babelscape/rebel) — BART-based seq2seq triple extractor, used by the LDK 2025 SOTA pipeline; actively cited, model on HF (check license: model card is CC BY-NC-SA; code separate). Adoption: candidate-statement generation booster for our parser (more triples in = more resolver chances), not a resolver replacement.
- **GIKS** (Penn State, Kim & Clariana; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09892-0) — live web tool converting essays to knowledge-structure graphs vs teacher referent; research access. Adoption: the algorithm (fixed key-term lexicon, sentence-window co-occurrence, Pathfinder pruning) is ~200 lines to reimplement and gives an edge-coverage signal that does not require parsed relational statements.
- **Kit-Build** (Hiroshima University; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s41039-015-0018-9) — adoption is the *design pattern* (closed kit / confirm-canonical-phrasing clarifications), not code.
- **IreneZihuiLi/CGPrompt** (https://github.com/IreneZihuiLi/CGPrompt) — LLM concept-graph recovery prompts + TutorQA benchmark; zero-shot GPT-4 edge recovery beat supervised baselines. Adoption: prompt patterns for edge-level probing.
- **Sentence-transformer proposition matcher** (per IndoBERT study, any modern bi-encoder) — adoption: new resolver tier "embed candidate statement vs node affirmative label(s), cosine ≥ τ", with τ tuned on our labeled 31-attempt corpus for recall at acceptable precision.

## Recall lessons

- **Bag-of-evidence over the whole artifact.** Classic scoring credits a proposition if it appears anywhere in the map/essay; ALA-Reader aggregates across all sentences. Any turn in the transcript should be able to credit a node; evidence should union across turns.
- **Fix the vocabulary, don't parse open text.** Every working automated system (ALA-Reader, GIKS, Kit-Build, selected-linking-phrases) constrains the term/link space first. For us: per-node alias/variant lexicons plus closed-set semantic matching; the open-extraction direction is the unsolved one.
- **Edges from co-occurrence, not joint parses.** Prior art credits a relation when both terms co-occur in a sentence/window (optionally Pathfinder-pruned). Replacing "both endpoints resolved within one parsed candidate statement" with "both endpoints resolved anywhere + co-occur within a k-sentence window" attacks the quadratic edge-recall collapse directly.
- **Thresholds: closed-set matching flips the base rate.** With ~5 reference nodes per problem, prior odds that a student statement maps to *some* node are high; the IndoBERT study got 100% recall at a mid cosine threshold (0.7). Precision-first NLI thresholds are calibrated for the wrong regime; tune per-tier thresholds on labeled data for recall, then let the graded-credit scale absorb residual uncertainty.
- **Partial credit absorbs matcher uncertainty.** The reliable human schemes rate proposition quality on 3–5 levels; a soft node score (e.g., exact=1.0, semantic=0.8, weak-mention=0.4) is truer to the tradition than a binary gate + abstention floor.
- **Constrain elicitation when matching fails.** Kit-Build and selected-linking-phrase results justify the clarification loop: presenting the canonical formulation and letting the student confirm converts an alignment problem into an exact match.
- **Calibrate against human proposition judgments.** Interrater reliability tops out around .9 and convergent validity with other measures is .3–.7; label a gold set of (statement, node) pairs from real transcripts and report resolver recall/precision against it — don't tune against intuition or end-to-end grades.

## Dead ends

- **Novak/Gowin structural scoring** (points for hierarchy levels ×5, cross-links ×10, etc.): lowest reliability among schemes (McClure et al.), assumes hierarchical structure that CRESST work showed is often invalid; our path-based coverage is already the better-validated convergence formulation.
- **Open-vocabulary map extraction then graph matching:** SOTA F1 28.5 in 2025 (LDK) — worse than our current problem, upstream of it.
- **Holistic LLM/RAG map grading as the score source:** QWK 0.146 vs experts (Kinetik 2026); use LLMs only inside per-node matching or clarification, never as the aggregate grader — consistent with our over-crediting transcript-audit.
- **Whole-map embedding similarity as the grade:** validated only against coarse similarity judgments; no per-node diagnosis, no partial credit (Sciarrone & Temperini 2024).
- **LSA-style latent semantic scoring of essays:** beaten by simple fixed-term co-occurrence (ALA-Reader r=.71 vs .56).
- **Adopting HIMATT/AKOVIA as software:** tools appear unmaintained/offline; take the seven-measure methodology, not the binaries.

Honest gaps: I found no study that measures *node-level recall of automated matchers on tutoring-dialogue transcripts* specifically (the literature uses maps or essays, not dialogue); the IndoBERT result is small-n (27 maps) and single-language; the exact r=.71 ALA-Reader figure comes from secondary summaries of Clariana's line of work and should be verified in Clariana, Koul & Salehi (Int. J. Instructional Media, 2006) before quoting it in anything durable.
