# Teachable agents & learning-by-teaching systems (Betty's Brain, SimStudent, and successors)

Research date: 2026-07-07. Question: how did learning-by-teaching (LBT) systems turn student teaching input into the agent's concept/causal map, and did any of them solve free-text -> canonical-node resolution (our resolver-recall problem)?

## Key findings

1. **Betty's Brain designed the resolution problem away — it never parses free text.** Students teach Betty by *directly manipulating* the causal map: a point-and-click/drag-and-drop editor creates concept nodes and links, with link semantics (increases/decreases) chosen from a pull-down menu (Blair, Schwartz, Biswas & Leelawong, "Pedagogical Agents for Learning by Teaching," 2007, https://aaalab.stanford.edu/assets/papers/2007/Pedagogical_agents_for_learning_by_teaching.pdf; Leelawong & Biswas, IJAIED 18(3) 2008, https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/2e83b05c5dbd26f54bd7dc59f7851d5771df34d4). Betty answers queries/quizzes by qualitative reasoning (causal chain propagation) over the *student's* map; the mentor agent grades quiz answers and the map itself by structural comparison against a hidden expert map (map score = matching causal links; CCMS/FCMS metrics in Munshi et al., arXiv:2202.09698, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2202.09698). Relevance: the flagship ancestor of Apollo grades in *structure space only* — student statements are already canonical because the interface forces them to be. There is no resolver, hence no resolver recall problem, hence no reusable resolution tech here — only the lesson that grading and NL understanding were deliberately decoupled.
2. **The "explicit, well-structured, shared visual representation" principle makes resolution interactive, not batch.** Blair & Schwartz's first TA design principle: the tutee's mind must be *visible* so the teacher can see and repair what the agent actually absorbed (same 2007 paper). Betty also spontaneously self-checks ("the answer I'm deriving doesn't seem to make sense"). Relevance: instead of silently mis-resolving a student statement and scoring it later, the agent should restate/display what it learned (in canonical terms) and let the student correct it live — resolution errors become dialogue turns, not grading errors.
3. **SimStudent never interpreted free text either: teaching input = demonstrations + yes/no feedback, knowledge = induced production rules.** SimStudent (Matsuda et al.) learns algebra skills by inductive logic programming (FOIL) over demonstrated steps and correctness feedback in APLUS; typed self-explanations in early studies were logged, not machine-understood (https://www.simstudent.org/home; "Teaching the Teacher," IJAIED 2015, https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40593-014-0020-1). Relevance: the second flagship also constrained the teaching channel to machine-checkable actions. Free-text understanding arrived only later, as follow-up-question scaffolding (finding 4), never as a grader.
4. **Shahriar & Matsuda's Constructive Tutee Inquiry is the closest worked-out clarification-loop architecture.** ("Can you clarify what you said?", AIED 2021, https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-78292-4_32; "What and How You Explain Matters," AIED 2023, https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-36272-9_11; arXiv:2508.18545). Pipeline: a Response Quality Classifier labels each tutor response (knowledge-telling vs knowledge-building); an Expected Response Generator produces the reference knowledge-building answer for the current inquiry; an Alignment Detector compares the student response to it with a **three-way output: aligned / not aligned / unable to detect**, which selects the next follow-up question from a fixed protocol. Treated students outperformed controls AND began knowledge-building unprompted over time. Relevance: this is our G2 clarification loop with a spec — per-node expected answers, alignment (not open matching), and an explicit "unable to detect" bucket routed to another question rather than to zero credit.
5. **The Curiosity Notebook NL extension is the only classic-line system that literally resolved free-text teaching into canonical nodes — and it reported no precision/recall.** Love, Law, Cohen & Kulić (arXiv:2203.09016, https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.09016; journal version IJAIED 2025, open access, https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00461-1) extended Curiosity Notebook (Law et al., CHI EA 2020, https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3334480.3382783) so students teach rock classification by typing. Mapping = Snips NLU intent classification + slot filling against **30 canonical rock features**, keeping every intent above a **20% confidence threshold** (multi-intent per input, recall-leaning); on NLU failure the agent says it didn't understand and offers retry, moving on after 3 failures. No P/R/F1 of the mapping was reported — only that ~20% of taught sentences hit the error path. Free-text vs sentence-selection tradeoff: free text = fewer teaching actions (12.4 vs 19.2), 2x slower per action (127s vs 72.5s), lower enjoyment, but significantly higher learning per teaching attempt (0.543 vs 0.357, p=.005) and higher perceived learning utility; paraphrasing amount correlated with learning. Relevance: direct precedent for a per-node matcher with a deliberately low threshold + explicit-failure retry loop; also a warning that this literature has no mapping-accuracy benchmark to copy.
6. **StuBot inverts the alignment problem: grade the pupil, not the alignment.** StuBot (Jin, Lee & Ko, EMNLP Findings 2022, https://aclanthology.org/2022.findings-emnlp.219/) treats the student's typed explanation as the agent's *entire knowledge*; a machine reading comprehension engine tries to answer a fixed bank of exam questions *from that text*. Questions it gets wrong/can't answer identify the "insufficient parts," and StuBot asks the student a follow-up about that key topic; iterate, then a final exam score shows teaching quality. Within-subject study (n=20): adaptive feedback significantly improved learning and immersion. Relevance: **node-first, query-directed checking over the whole transcript** instead of statement-first parse->match. Our recall is capped by the parser's candidate extraction; StuBot's pattern (per-node probe questions answered by QA/NLI over the raw transcript, with abstention) sidesteps that cap and naturally handles multi-sentence, indirect teaching.
7. **LLM-era tutees track coverage in-context and are documented to over-credit — corroborating our transcript-audit failure.** (a) Ruffle&Riley (Schmucker et al., arXiv:2310.01420, https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01420; AIED 2024 version arXiv:2404.17460, https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.17460) auto-induces a tutoring script from lesson text via GPT-4 (review questions -> solutions -> **expectations**), then instructs the tutee agent to keep asking follow-ups "until all expectations are covered," with the chat log as the only state. Observed failures: conversations omitted 1-2 expectations, and the system "was often lenient towards user responses that only covered parts of one expectation"; N=200 studies found no learning gain vs reading despite higher engagement. Descended from AutoTutor's expectation-misconception tailoring but with misconception detection delegated to GPT-4 at runtime. (b) TeachYou/AlgoBo (Jin, Lee, Shin & Kim, CHI 2024, https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.14534) stores AlgoBo's knowledge state as JSON (facts + code_implementation) updated by a Reflect-Respond GPT-4 pipeline — no canonical knowledge-component mapping; reported problems: partially incorrect teaching still improved AlgoBo's quiz scores, the LLM drifted back toward correct knowledge (erasing prescribed misconceptions), and a fine-tuned response-quality classifier managed only 71.3% accuracy. Relevance: two independent replications of our finding that LLM-in-the-loop coverage judgment is over-lenient — use it to drive dialogue, never as the score source.
8. **Menu-constrained vs free-text is a measured tradeoff, not dogma.** Constrained input (Betty's map editor; Curiosity Notebook sentence-selection; Pareto's menu-based math game, IJAIED 2014, https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40593-014-0018-8; Iwase et al. 2021 predefined-sentence dialogue) gives full machine-readability and higher coverage per session; free text gives deeper per-action learning and higher perceived value at the cost of speed, enjoyment, and machine understanding (finding 5; StuBot's motivation section makes the same argument, and a baseline study in AlgoBo found only 2.7% of learner messages were knowledge-building without scaffolds). Recent deployments at scale (MatlabTutee, CHI 2025 "Playing Dumb to Get Smart," https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3706598.3713644, 119 students; "Turning 500+ Students into Teachers," L@S 2026, https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3774398.3811623) all went fully free-text with LLM tutees and measure engagement/learning, not resolution fidelity. Relevance: our free-text choice is the field's current direction; nobody at scale has paired it with trustworthy canonical-node grading — that pairing is exactly our gap.

## Adoptable artifacts

- **Snips NLU** — https://github.com/snipsco/snips-nlu (Apache 2.0, **unmaintained/inactive** since ~2019-2021). What Curiosity Notebook used for per-node intent+slot matching. Not worth adopting as code in 2026, but its *configuration* transfers: per-node classifier, low (0.2) confidence cutoff, multiple accepted intents per utterance.
- **Constructive Tutee Inquiry protocol** (Shahriar & Matsuda) — no public repo found; the AIED 2023 paper + arXiv:2508.18545 specify the components (response classifier trained with LightSIDE-style features, expected-response generation, 3-way alignment detector, fixed follow-up question protocol). Adoption = re-implement with an LLM as expected-response generator; our per-node `content.label` already gives the reference text.
- **Ruffle&Riley script-induction prompts** (arXiv:2404.17460 describes the 4-step GPT-4 chain: extract review questions -> generate solutions -> derive expectations -> compile script). Adoption = auto-generate 1-3 probe questions + expected answers per reference node from our problem resources, reviewed by a human, instead of hand-authoring NLI labels.
- **TeachYou/AlgoBo** — demo + materials at https://teachyou.kixlab.org (KIXLAB, KAIST; org repo https://github.com/kixlab — paper says "samples available at our GitHub," specific repo not verified). Adoption target: the Reflect-Respond pattern (respond ONLY from retrieved taught-knowledge; say "I'm not sure how to do that. Could you explain it to me?" on retrieval miss) is a good behavioral spec for Apollo-the-pupil that doubles as a clarification trigger.
- **StuBot pattern** — no code released (EMNLP Findings 2022 paper only). Adoption = per-node probe-question QA over the raw transcript with abstention (extractive QA or our existing NLI tier pointed at the transcript window instead of parsed candidates), scored as node covered iff the probe is answered correctly.
- **Betty's Brain** — hosted by Vanderbilt OELE (https://www.vanderbilt.edu/oele/bettys-brain/, https://bettysbrain.teachableagents.org); not open source. Adoption is the *pattern*: show the student Apollo's current "understanding map" (resolved nodes/edges) and let them correct it — converts resolver misses into visible, fixable state.

## Recall lessons

- **Go node-first, not statement-first.** Every system that checked free-text coverage did it query-directed: StuBot asks "can the exam question be answered from the explanation?"; Ruffle&Riley checks per-expectation; Shahriar & Matsuda compare against a per-inquiry expected response. None ran a parser then aligned extracted statements to nodes. Our node_coverage recall is capped by parser candidate recall (then compounded quadratically for edges); running the per-node check (NLI/QA/probe question) directly over the transcript removes that cap and catches teaching spread across multiple turns.
- **Recall-leaning thresholds + explicit failure + retry.** Curiosity Notebook accepted every intent above 0.2 confidence and, on failure, *told the student* it didn't understand and let them retry (3x). The worst design is ours-before-clarification: silently dropping unmatched statements. An "unable to detect" outcome should always route to a clarification turn (Shahriar & Matsuda's third class), never straight to a zero.
- **Make resolution visible and let the student repair it.** Betty's Brain principle: the agent's absorbed knowledge is always on screen; teaching = iteratively fixing it. For us: Apollo restates what it just learned in near-canonical terms ("So pressure drops when velocity rises — did I get that right?"); a confirmation resolves the node at high confidence, a correction catches a mis-resolution. This is both the recall fix and good pedagogy (the protégé-effect literature says checking the tutee's understanding is where tutor learning happens).
- **Keep the lenient judge out of the score.** Ruffle&Riley and AlgoBo show LLM coverage judgment inflates credit (partial coverage accepted, misconceptions self-heal). Betty's Brain shows the alternative: dialogue can be as fuzzy as it likes, but *the score* is computed by deterministic comparison in structure space. Use LLM/NLI signals to trigger clarification; let only confirmed (student-verified or high-precision-tier) resolutions into node_coverage.
- **Partial credit is per-link/per-node structural counting** (Betty's map score counts each correct causal link; CCMS updates per edit) — consistent with our covered/total design; the literature offers no support for holistic LLM ratings as the grade.
- **Honest gap:** no paper in this entire lineage reports precision/recall of free-text->node mapping. The field optimizes learning outcomes, where a mis-resolution costs one confused dialogue turn; in our grading use it costs points. Expect to build our own resolution benchmark (our 31-attempt corpus is already more measurement than any of these systems published).

## Dead ends

- **Menu/sentence-selection-only teaching input.** It solves resolution by construction (Betty, Pareto, Iwase) but measurably reduces per-action learning and generative effort vs free text (Love et al. 2025), and contradicts Apollo's dialogue product. Hybrid confirmation (agent restates; student clicks yes/no) keeps the benefit without the cost.
- **Trusting the LLM tutee's internal knowledge-state updates as the grader.** AlgoBo documents unvalidated mapping, credit for wrong teaching, and drift back to correct knowledge. Same failure class as our over-crediting transcript audit.
- **Chat-log-as-state expectation tracking for scoring** (Ruffle&Riley): omits expectations, lenient on partial coverage; fine for steering conversation, unusable as a score source.
- **Small fine-tuned response classifiers as grading gates.** 71.3% accuracy (AlgoBo's quality classifier; similar for its Teaching Helper) is fine for choosing the next dialogue move, far below what a grade needs.
- **Mining this literature for resolution accuracy benchmarks or tuned thresholds.** They don't exist here (see honest gap); the transferable assets are architectures and interaction patterns, not calibrated numbers.
